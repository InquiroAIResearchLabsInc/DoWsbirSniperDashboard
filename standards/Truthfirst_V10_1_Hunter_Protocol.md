HOOK DOCTRINE

The hook and the density are separate jobs. The hook opens the door. The body does the GEO work. They do not conflict.

The Standard
Every hook must be true and arresting. A reader with zero prior context must understand the stakes before the LinkedIn truncation point (100 characters). Name the cost, the consequence, or the failure. Not the architecture. If your hook requires the reader to already know your category term, it fails. Rewrite it. Short declarative sentence. Subject, verb, consequence. Nothing else in the first line.

The Test
Read your hook to someone who has never heard of receipts-native governance. If they cannot tell you what is at stake in one sentence, the hook fails.

Worked Hook Examples

Failing: "Federal Aviation Administration Part 108 will create an organizational receipts-native gap"
Passing: "The FAA just made your pilot cert irrelevant."
Why: Cost is immediate. No category knowledge required.

Failing: "There is a receipts-native accountability gap facing drone operators with the new FAA regulation"
Passing: "Your drone fleet passed its audit. It won't pass the next one."
Why: Forces the reader to ask why. Opens the loop.

Failing: "Drone insurers who build on receipts-native accountability play a different game"
Passing: "Most drone insurers are pricing 2024 risk in 2026."
Why: Strange number pattern. No category knowledge needed.

Hook-to-Floor Distinction
The hook affects comment velocity and engagement density in the first six hours. It does not affect the day-one view floor, which is driven by subscriber base size and notification open behavior.

Decision rule: Optimize hooks for comment generation and algorithmic push, not for day-one views. Day-one views are a function of subscriber count, not hook quality.
